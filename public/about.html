Why is that the direction?

Bearing points you along the great circle: the shortest path between you and the city over the surface of the Earth. Stretch a string between two points on a globe and pull it taut — the line it settles on is a great circle. Airplanes fly these routes for the same reason.

The straight line you'd draw on a flat map is something else: a rhumb line, a path of constant compass bearing. Flat maps (like the Mercator projection) have to stretch the globe to flatten it — increasingly so toward the poles — and that stretching turns the truly shortest path into a curve, while making the longer constant-bearing path look deceptively straight.

London → Los Angeles: the great circle is 8,760 km and leaves London pointing northwest (312°), up over Greenland. The rhumb line is 9,730 km — about 11% longer — pointing west-southwest (258°). Same two cities, 54° apart at the start.

What it means for your score

When you point your phone, Bearing compares your direction with the great circle's initial bearing. So from Los Angeles, Tokyo is northwest (306°), not "west across the Pacific" — and from London, Singapore starts out just north of due east (78°), not southeast. Trust the globe in your head, not the wall map.
